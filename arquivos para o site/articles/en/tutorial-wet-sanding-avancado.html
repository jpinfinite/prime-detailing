Wet Sanding: Advanced Correction Technique

Learn the wet sanding technique for correcting severe defects. When to use it, correct grits and essential care.

What is Wet Sanding?

Wet sanding is the most aggressive paint correction technique. It uses fine grit sandpaper with water to remove a controlled layer of clear coat, correcting defects that polishing cannot.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL NOTICE

Wet sanding is EXTREMELY risky. One mistake can get through the clear coat and ruin the paintwork permanently. DO NOT attempt without proper training and a thickness gauge.

Removes material:

  • Sandpaper 1500: 5-10 microns per pass
  • Sandpaper 2000: 3-5 microns per pass
  • Sandpaper 3000: 1-3 microns per pass
  • Total clear coat: 40-60 microns

Conclusion: You have a VERY small margin for error!

When to Use Wet Sanding

use wet sanding for:

  • Severe orange peel
  • Very deep scratches in the clear coat
  • Chemically etched water spots
  • Paint overspray
  • Leveling after retouching
  • Removing damaged clear coat

do NOT use wet sanding for:

  • Swirl marks (use polish)
  • Light scratches (use compound)
  • Oxidation (use compound)
  • Holograms (use finishing polish)
  • First correction attempt

üí° Golden rule

Wet sanding is the LAST resort. Always try aggressive compounding first. If compound doesn't work, then consider wet sanding.

Choice of grits

1200 Grit (Very Aggressive)

  • Use: Severe overspray, very damaged clear coat
  • Removes: 10-15 microns per pass
  • Risk: VERY HIGH
  • Recommendation: Experienced professionals only

1500 Grit (Aggressive)

  • Use: Severe orange peel, deep scratches
  • Removes: 5-10 microns per pass
  • Risk: High
  • Recommendation: Experienced professionals

2000 Grit (Moderate)

  • Use: Moderate orange peel, water spots
  • Removes: 3-5 microns per pass
  • Risk: Medium
  • Recommendation: Starting point for beginners

2500 Grit (Light)

  • Use: Refinishing after 1500/2000
  • Removes: 2-3 microns per pass
  • Risk: Low-medium

3000 Grit (Very Light)

  • Use: Final finish before polish
  • Removes: 1-2 microns per pass
  • Risk: Low
  • Recommendation: Always use before polishing

Step-by-step process

Preparation (30 minutes)

  1. Wash thoroughly: Remove all dirt
  2. Dry: Surface must be dry
  3. Measure thickness: At 10+ points in the area
  4. Document: Write down all readings
  5. Calculate margin: Thickness - 30 microns = safe margin
  6. Mark area: Crepe tape delimiting
  7. Prepare solution: Water + 2-3 drops of shampoo

Test (15 minutes)

  1. Choose small, discreet area (5x5cm)
  2. Measure initial thickness
  3. Make 10 passes with 2000 grit
  4. Measure again
  5. Calculate removal per pass
  6. Adjust technique if necessary

Wet Sanding (1-3 hours)

Correct technique

  1. Wet sandpaper: Soak in water for 5 minutes
  2. Wet surface: Spray generously
  3. Pressure: Light to medium (leave weight of club)
  4. Movement: Straight, horizontal
  5. Passes: 10-15 in one direction
  6. Rotation: Rotate 90¬∞, plus 10-15 passes
  7. Keep wet: Constant spray
  8. Clean frequently: Remove residue
  9. Inspect: Every 20-30 passes
  10. Measure: Every 50 passes

Grit Progression

  1. 1500 grit: Until defectis removed (if necessary)
  2. 2000 grit: Refine marks from 1500
  3. 2500 grit: Refine marks from 2000 (optional)
  4. 3000 grit: Final finish

  • ‚ùå Color change (hit base coat)
  • different texture (crossed clear)
  • thickness below 30 microns
  • excessive heat (friction without water)

If any of these occur: STOP IMMEDIATELY!

Refining and Polishing

Wet sanding leaves the surface matte and with sandpaper marks. Polishing is a MUST.

Stage 1: Aggressive Compound

  • Pad: Orange (cutting)
  • Product: Meguiar's M105 or 3M Perfect-It III
  • Speed: 5-6
  • Passes: 6-8 in each direction
  • Objective: Remove sanding marks

Step 2: Medium Polish

  • Pad: White (polishing)
  • Product: Meguiar's M205 or Scholl S3
  • Speed: 4-5
  • Passes: 4-6 in each direction
  • Objective: Refine finish

Step 3: Finishing Polish

  • Pad: Black (finishing)
  • Product: Meguiar's M210 or 3M Ultrafina
  • Speed: 3-4
  • Passes: 3-4 in each direction
  • Objective: Final mirror shine

Specific Use Cases

Orange Peel

Problem: Wavy texture on paintwork

Solution:

  1. Measure thickness (need minimum 50 microns)
  2. Wet sand with 2000 grit until level
  3. Refine with 3000 grit
  4. Complete polish (3 steps)
  5. Result: Glass-smooth surface

Engraved Water Spots

Problem: Water stains that won't come off with polish

Solution:

  1. Try aggressive compound first
  2. If it doesn't come off: wet sand with 2000 grit
  3. Only in the affected area
  4. Blend (transition) to surrounding area
  5. Full Polish

Paint Overspray

Problem: Paint splashes from another car/work

Solution:

  1. Try clay bar first
  2. If it doesn't come off: wet sand with 1500-2000 grit
  3. Be careful not to sand too much
  4. Full polish after

Fatal mistakes to avoid

  1. Don't measure thickness: Can go through clear coat
  2. Dry sanding: Excessive heat, deep scratches
  3. Too much pressure: Removes material too quickly
  4. Don't use a tack: Fingers create low spots
  5. Dirty sandpaper: Particles cause deep scratches
  6. Don't progress grits: Sandpaper marks don't come off
  7. Skip polishing: Surface becomes matte
  8. Working on corners: Thinner clear coat
  9. Hurry: Wet sanding requires patience
  10. First time on expensive car: Practice on scrap first

Alternatives to Wet Sanding

Before wet sanding, consider:

1. Aggressive Compound with Rotary

  • Less risky
  • Removes 70-80% of defects
  • May be sufficient

2. Multiple Polishing Stages

  • Heavy compound ‚Üí medium ‚Üí light
  • Longer, but safer
  • Preserves more clear coat

3. Accept limitations

  • Some defects are not worth the risk
  • 80% improvement is already excellent
  • Perfection is not always possible

Conclusion

Wet sanding is a powerful but dangerous tool. It can turn ruined paintwork into a mirror, but it can also destroy clear coat permanently.

Before you try it:
